Sit At My Table

David Cannon Wilson is a mystic, poet, and all around great friend of mine. Recently I was able to illustrate for his upcoming podcast called "Sit At My Table", where he will be interviewing a variety of guests to talk about life and how we live it. His sincere search for truth is contagious. His brute honesty is admirable. If you want to be challenged for an hour or so please check out this provocative podcast. You can follow David on instagram @dcwilsonspeaks or check out his older podcast Muddy Water by clicking the links below.

The Process (2019)

David Cannon Wilson

Personal. Confident. Welcoming.

The illustration says it all. I wanted to capture David's persona with a very minimilistic style using digital penwork. I focused on his expression while maintaining a very simple and clean look. I complimented the precision with a slightly lazy outline of his suit.

I wanted the typography to look handwritten to match the penwork. I chose a marker style font called Marker Felt.

Reference Photo

I used one of David's Facebook pictures as a reference. I loved this angle as I imagined him sitting at a desk. His facial expressions also match the messages I wanted to send in the illustration.

Beside him is his wife, Dustina. Aren't they just the cutest couple ever!?

Sketch to Digital

For a podcast called Sit At My Table, I immediately thought, "The illustration needs to be personal, confident, and inviting". The idea is simple really and it works great for a podcast.

I quickly sketched a thumbnail of the concept to David. At first he was hesitant but eventually he saw the value in the visual message. Personal, confident, and inviting. These are the elements I wanted to spark with the illustration.

I'll be honest, the sketch doesn't show those three elements very well. It's so small and rough that I didn't quite sell the idea to David yet. I went to the iPad and made the necessary adjustments.

Three things to notice: the facial expression, the posture, and the up-close-and-personal-ness of the frame. There's enough space in the image as well so it doesn't overwhelm you. The balance between the object and the words is pleasing to the eye.
